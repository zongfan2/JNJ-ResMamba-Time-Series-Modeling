Internal Research Proposal · Deep TSO (IMWUT Paper 2)

Deep TSO: Noise-Robust, Structured Sleep-Window Detection from Raw Accelerometry

Reframing minute-level TSO prediction as learning-from-noisy-labels with a single-interval structural prior and cross-night personalization.

Prepared for manager discussion · Builds on training/train_tso_patch_h5.py & MBA4TSO_Patch · Draft v1

Executive summary

We currently predict minute-level sleep/non-sleep over a full day with a Mamba model trained by plain cross-entropy against labels produced by a traditional TSO algorithm. Those labels are not ground truth — they are noisy, and the noise is structured (concentrated at sleep onset/offset). Three changes turn this into a defensible novel contribution:

  1. Learn from noisy labels explicitly. Replace plain CE with a robust loss (Generalized Cross Entropy) and treat multiple traditional algorithms as independent noisy annotators, weighting the loss by their agreement.
  2. Use prior knowledge as supervision. A subject's nights are similar — add a cross-night contrastive objective so the encoder learns sleep structure, not the labeler's mistakes.
  3. Predict structure, not pixels. Enforce a single contiguous TSO interval (CRF / interval head) instead of independent per-minute decisions.

Most of the machinery (GCE, SupCon, a continuity loss, a per-minute encoder) already exists in the repo. DivideMix-style clean/noisy splitting is intentionally out of scope for this version.

1. Where we are, and why it limits us

The pipeline below takes a whole day of raw 20 Hz accelerometer + temperature data, embeds one-minute patches, models time with a Mamba state-space encoder, and emits an independent label per minute.

Figure 1 — Current Deep TSO pipeline. The red box is the weak point: plain cross-entropy treats noisy labels as if they were ground truth.

Three structural weaknesses follow from this design:

  • Labels are treated as truth. measure_loss_tso uses plain BCE/CE. Deep networks fit clean patterns first and memorize label noise later, so the model actively learns the traditional algorithm's errors.
  • Each minute is decided independently. TSO is physiologically a single contiguous block per night, but the model can emit fragmented predictions; only a post-hoc smoother enforces continuity.
  • Model selection is on noisy validation loss. Early stopping monitors val_loss against noisy labels and dataset_test = dataset_val — so "best" currently means "best mimic of the traditional algorithm."

2. The key insight: the noise is structured

Traditional sleep/TSO algorithms (Sadeh, Cole–Kripke, van Hees HDCZA) agree strongly on the interior of sleep and disagree mainly at the onset/offset boundaries and during quiet wakefulness. This is the single most important fact for the design — it tells us exactly where the labels can and cannot be trusted.

Figure 2 — Three labelers agree on the sleep core (green, high-confidence) but disagree at the edges (amber). The plain-CE model wastes capacity fitting amber minutes that are essentially label noise.
Design consequence. If we can measure labeler agreement, we get a free, per-minute confidence signal: full supervision where algorithms agree, soft / down-weighted supervision where they disagree. This drives Innovation 1.

3. Innovation 1 — Learning from noisy labels

Two complementary mechanisms, both low-risk and largely drop-in given existing code.

aRobust loss instead of plain CE

Swap the BCE/CE inside measure_loss_tso for Generalized Cross Entropy (GCE) — already implemented as GCELoss in losses/standard.py but unused for TSO. GCE interpolates between CE (fast but noise-sensitive) and MAE (noise-robust) via a single parameter q:

Lq(p, y) = (1 − pyq) / q   # q→0 ≈ cross-entropy · q=1 = MAE (most robust)

Symmetric Cross Entropy (SCE) is an equally valid alternative. This is a one-function change with no architectural impact and directly reduces noise memorization.

bMultiple traditional algorithms as noisy annotators

Rather than one label track, run 2–3 traditional algorithms and treat each as an independent annotator with its own biases. Where they agree, supervise fully; where they disagree, down-weight (agreement-as-confidence) or learn each annotator's reliability with a crowd layer / noise-adaptation layer that maps a shared clean posterior to each annotator's noisy labels.

Figure 3 — Agreement across labelers yields a per-minute weight wi; the model is supervised strongly on the core and weakly at the contested edges.

This framing is genuinely novel for actigraphy TSO — the literature almost never models the traditional algorithms as a crowd of annotators — and it matches the structured-noise picture in Figure 2 exactly.

4. Innovation 2 — Prior knowledge as supervision (cross-night contrastive)

The manager's intuition — the same person sleeps similarly across nights — is a free supervisory signal that does not depend on the noisy labels at all. We encode it with a contrastive objective on a per-night embedding: nights from the same subject attract, nights from different subjects repel.

Figure 4 — Supervised contrastive loss with subject ID as the positive key. SupConLoss already exists in the repo; we add an optional pooled embedding to MBA4TSO_Patch.forward and a small projection head.

Why it helps under label noise

The objective is grounded in the data (who recorded the night), not in the noisy labels, so it regularizes the representation toward real sleep structure and away from labeler artifacts. It pairs naturally with self-supervised contrastive pretraining (TS-TCC / TF-C) on the large pool of unlabeled wear-time.

Two guardrails

Avoid the identity shortcut: keep the contrastive loss on a separate projection head with a modest weight (≈0.1–0.3), so the model encodes sleep structure rather than re-identifying subjects. Tie it to TSO: optionally add an intra-subject consistency penalty on predicted onset/duration variance — the prior expressed in the output space where it matters.

5. Innovation 3 — Predict structure, not pixels

Because TSO is a single contiguous interval, the strongest output-side change is to stop making 1,440 independent decisions and instead enforce one block. This both raises accuracy and produces clinically meaningful output (one onset, one offset).

Figure 5 — Moving the single-interval prior from a post-processing heuristic into the model. Boundary-uncertainty (evidential) heads can additionally report calibrated confidence at the contested edges.

6. Validation without TSO ground truth

We do not have reliable TSO ground truth, so we cannot directly score sleep-window accuracy. The fix is not one metric but a three-tier strategy — a downstream proxy that measures what TSO is for, a label-free consistency check, and a small gold set to anchor both.

The problem with the status quo. Early stopping currently runs on noisy val_loss and dataset_test = dataset_val, so "best model" today means "best mimic of the traditional algorithm." None of the three innovations is provable until this changes.

1Downstream proxy — scratch detection (primary)

TSO exists to gate/normalize nocturnal scratch quantification, so a better TSO should yield better downstream scratch metrics. Crucially, our scratch ground-truth labels are independent of the TSO window (annotated over the full recording, not inside the traditional algorithm's sleep window), so this proxy is not circular — improving downstream scratch performance cannot be achieved merely by agreeing with the traditional TSO.

Experimental design: hold the finished Deep Scratch model fixed, swap only the TSO source feeding it, and report scratch metrics on nights with scratch GT. This isolates TSO quality as the single moving part.

Figure 6 — Extrinsic validation. Because scratch GT is independent of TSO, downstream gains reflect genuine TSO improvement, not agreement with the traditional labeler.

Sensitivity caveat: most scratch events sit in the sleep interior, so blunt window-agnostic metrics may barely move under boundary errors. Use TSO-normalized metrics (scratch index per hour of TSO, total scratch time within window) and inspect nights where scratch occurs near the edges.

2Cross-night consistency (label-free)

A correct TSO should give the same subject consistent onset/offset/duration across nights. Low intra-subject variance is therefore weak evidence of quality with no labels at all — and it is the same prior as Innovation 2, now doing double duty as a validation signal.

3Small gold-standard anchor

Even 10–20 expert- or PSG-scored nights, reserved purely for model selection and final reporting, calibrate the proxy and catch target drift (a TSO that helps scratch but drifts from physiological sleep). Proxy + small gold set is far stronger than either alone. This is the one remaining data question for the manager.
